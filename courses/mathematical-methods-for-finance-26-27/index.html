Mathematical Methods for Finance

This course develops the mathematical foundations required for advanced study in quantitative finance. It introduces functional analysis, measure-theoretic probability and stochastic calculus, with particular attention to the tools used in asset pricing, risk theory and financial modelling.

Institution University of Perugia

Welcome to the course

The course is mathematically demanding, but it is designed for students in economics who have chosen a quantitative finance programme. Formal theory will be systematically accompanied by worked examples, guided exercises and financial applications.

Preparation

Prerequisites and mathematical bridge

Students are expected to be familiar with the following topics at an elementary undergraduate level:

  • algebraic manipulation, logarithms and exponential functions;
  • limits, derivatives and elementary integration;
  • sequences, series and basic convergence concepts;
  • vectors, matrices and elementary linear algebra;
  • elementary probability and random variables;
  • standard mathematical notation and logical reasoning.

Diagnostic and revision material

Before the course begins, this section will contain a non-graded diagnostic test, complete solutions and concise revision notes. The purpose is to identify gaps early and provide a clear route for independent revision.

Objectives

Learning outcomes

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

  • use metric, normed, Banach and Hilbert space concepts in mathematical and financial problems;
  • work with measures, measurable functions, Lebesgue integration and the main convergence theorems;
  • analyse random variables, conditional expectations and different modes of convergence;
  • apply characteristic functions, the law of large numbers and the central limit theorem;
  • describe stochastic processes, filtrations, martingales and Brownian motion;
  • compute quadratic variations and stochastic integrals and apply Itô's formula;
  • interpret changes of measure, stochastic differential equations and Feynman–Kac formulas in quantitative finance;
  • present mathematical arguments and solutions clearly, rigorously and efficiently.

Syllabus

Course roadmap

The roadmap is organised by mathematical progression, not by calendar week. The allocation of teaching hours will be finalised after publication of the official timetable.

Course modules View the complete course structure
Module 0 Mathematical bridge and diagnostic review

Core topics: notation, elementary calculus, sequences, matrices and probability review.

Learning purpose: establish a common mathematical starting point and identify prerequisites that require revision.

Practice: diagnostic questions and guided revision exercises.

Module 1 Metric, normed and Hilbert spaces

Core topics: metric spaces, convergence, completeness, compactness, normed spaces, Banach spaces, Hölder and Minkowski inequalities, Hilbert spaces, orthogonality, projection and Bessel inequality.

Selected extensions: sequence spaces, function spaces, smooth functions and linear operators.

Practice focus: verifying metrics and norms, studying convergence and completeness, orthogonal projections and approximation problems.

Module 2 Measure theory, integration and Fourier methods

Core topics: sigma-algebras, Borel sets, measures, measurable functions, Lebesgue integral, integrable functions, equivalence classes, monotone convergence, Fatou's lemma, dominated convergence and Lp spaces.

Fourier topics: trigonometric polynomials, Fourier series, Fourier transform, Plancherel theorem and convolution.

Selected extensions: Schwartz spaces and the Fast Fourier Transform.

Module 3 Probability and conditional methods

Core topics: probability spaces, random variables, distributions, expectation, variance, covariance, correlation, independence, conditional probability, conditional distribution and conditional expectation.

Analytical tools: Jensen inequality, Fubini theorem, characteristic functions, inversion and Radon–Nikodym methods.

Applications: Cholesky decomposition, Bayes formula and construction of conditional marginals.

Module 4 Convergence and limit theorems

Core topics: sequences of random variables, almost sure, probability, mean-square and weak convergence, Markov inequality, law of large numbers and central limit theorem.

Further tools: necessary and sufficient conditions for weak convergence and Lévy's continuity theorem.

Practice focus: distinguishing convergence concepts and selecting the appropriate theorem in probabilistic problems.

Module 5 Stochastic processes, martingales and Brownian motion

Core topics: stochastic processes, finite-dimensional distributions, measurable processes, filtrations, martingales, Markov processes, Brownian motion, Wiener space, stopping times and Poisson processes.

Selected extensions: Feller processes, Chapman–Kolmogorov equations, forward and backward Kolmogorov equations, and classes of continuous and càdlàg martingales.

Module 6 Stochastic integration and Itô calculus

Core topics: variation, Riemann–Stieltjes integration, semimartingales, quadratic variation, stochastic integration, Itô processes and Itô's formula.

Development: integration of indicator and simple processes, construction in L2, local L2 methods and integration with respect to continuous semimartingales.

Applications: Brownian and multidimensional Itô formulas.

Module 7 Measure changes, SDEs and financial applications

Core topics: exponential martingales, Girsanov theorem, stochastic differential equations and Feynman–Kac formulas.

Selected extensions: Lévy characterization, Brownian martingale representation and Least Squares Monte Carlo.

Financial perspective: risk-neutral dynamics, valuation problems and links with asset pricing and risk theory.

Module 8 Consolidation and examination preparation

Integrated exercises, links among the three parts of the course, examination guidance and at least one complete mock written examination.
